A walk through the grounds.

境内案内　—　GROUNDS
The Grounds

Enter through the mountain gate — and walk upward, slowly.

Myohokkein is built into the slope, rising from street level through a five-ring arch, a short bridge, and a gently climbing ramp to the main hall above. What follows is a guide to twenty places on the grounds, in the order a visitor would meet them.

Mountain Gate

山門（正門）— Sanmon

The main gate at street level. The inscription Shōchū-zan is a transcription of calligraphy by the 17th-century master Hon'ami Kōetsu, copied from the tablet at the head temple Hokkekyō-ji in Chiba.

The Five-Ring Gate

五重門 — Gojū-mon

A gate cut as a perfect circle, with the passage (Ryōzen Bridge) running tangent rather than through the centre — an unusual design.

From the outside inward, the five rings are named Bukkyō, Daijō, Hokke, Honmon, and Daimoku — ten steps into the world of the Lotus Sūtra. On the way out, they become the five characters of the Sūtra's title itself: myō · hō · ren · ge · kyō.

Ryōzen Bridge

霊山橋 — Ryōzen-bashi

Named after the bridges at Mt. Minobu and Ikegami Honmon-ji, this six-metre span threads the centre of the Five-Ring Gate. Crossing it feels like stepping into the Pure Land of Eagle Peak — the sacred precinct where the Buddha expounded the Lotus.

Shikyō-Nanji Slope

此経難持坂 — Shikyō-Nanji-zaka

After the gate and the bridge, a gentle ramp spirals upward around the inner courtyard to the second and third floors — wheelchair-accessible throughout.

It echoes the Shikyō-Nanji stone steps at Ikegami Honmon-ji: 96 black stones are set into the pavement, matching the syllable count of the Hōtō-ge verse.

The passage between the five-storey office wing and the corridor wing shifted a few centimetres — vertically and laterally — during the 1995 earthquake.

Peace Memorial Pagoda

平和供養塔 — Heiwa Kuyō-tō

A central pagoda honouring the victims of all wars, flanked by four smaller pagodas for the victims of air raids and the atomic bombings.

In the centre burns the Flame of Peace — brought here on 8 April 1988 while it was being relayed to New York for the Third UN Special Session on Disarmament (SSD III). It has burned uninterrupted ever since.

06 · Main Hall
06 / 20

The Main Hall
Jusen-dō

本堂（鷲山堂）— Hondō / Jusen-dō

The outer hall seats about 120 on chairs and can also serve as a concert hall. The inner sanctuary is raised one step and opens upward through the second and third floors into a domed ceiling.

The great altar is stunning: a ten-by-seven-metre black-and-gold Daimandara mandala covers the rear wall, modelled on the mandala granted by Nichiren Shōnin at the moment of his death. Before it rises an eight-metre Treasure Pagoda, with Śākyamuni and Prabhūtaratna seated at its top, the four bodhisattvas springing from the earth, and a 400-year-old statue of Nichiren Shōnin (attributed to Rev. Chūshō-in Nichigo) floating above a lotus platform.

Below them, a monument of light symbolises the universe; a sphere represents our water planet, guarded by the Four Heavenly Kings on a black octagonal base. The whole forms a five-ring pagoda. The octagonal base is called the Shōkyō-den, and it stores transcriptions of Nichiren's autograph writings alongside sūtras hand-copied by the congregation.

Mortuary Hall

納骨堂 — Nōkotsudō

Completed in April 2004 — a three-storey reinforced concrete building. The ground floor is let to tenants; the second and third house the hall itself.

At the front rises a five-metre-tall Daimandara and an Ittō-ryōson (one-pagoda-two-buddhas) statue, modelled on the same mandala as the main hall. The interior holds about 200 private ossuary altars, with separate perpetual-care pagodas and shared altars for remains and memorial tablets.

Training Hall
Kokū-dō

道場（虚空堂）— Kokū-dō

On the third floor, above the outer hall, a hall for faith-practice and study — set at exactly the height of the buddhas and bodhisattvas on the Treasure Pagoda below, so that one can face them eye-to-eye. This placement mirrors the Lotus Sūtra's Ceremony in the Air.

Water Ablution Place

水行場 — Suigyō-jō

A small chamber for the traditional practice of suigyō — pouring buckets of cold water over oneself while chanting the Kanmon. Performed every morning, including through the coldest months of winter.

A water-deity is enshrined at the centre of the tank; three practitioners can carry out the rite at once.

Prayer Hall

祈祷所 — Kitō-jo

On the ground floor, directly beneath the inner sanctuary of the main hall. A heavy door bears the two characters hi-myō ("hidden marvel") in calligraphy.

The central figure is a statue of Kishimojin, borne through more than 120 years of Great-Ascetic-Hall training under three successive abbots. Prayer rites are offered here; visitors can perform the o-hyakudo hundred-walk vow around it, and even automobile blessings are possible.

Meeting Room

会議室 — Kaigi-shitsu

On the third floor — usable as a rentable meeting room, green room, or dining hall. A small kitchen occupies one corner, and a service lift connects to the second and fourth floors. Suited to meetings and seminars of up to about twenty people.

Tatami Hall

和室広間 — Washitsu Hiroma

On the fourth floor. Two adjoining rooms that can be opened into a single 36-mat hall by removing the fusuma sliding doors — suitable for post-memorial meals and for residential study retreats.

Entrance

エントランス — Entoransu

The street-level corner of the temple, with a wooden door. Visitors using wheelchairs or with limited mobility can enter here and ride the lift from the ground floor. The glazed frontage also allows the space to serve as a small exhibition room.

Temple Office

事務所 — Jimusho

Set on the second floor, a glazed room reached by the ramp. Reception, administration, and the temple's operational nerve centre — and home to the local committee of Hyogo's "Passing On the Stories of War" exhibition society.

Car Park

駐車場 — Chūshajō

Adjacent to the road, with easy in-and-out access. Five cars in normal use; the prayer-hall forecourt opens up for additional parking during memorial services.

Please note: the two spaces furthest left are leased out on weekdays and unavailable; on weekends they may be used.

The Two Domes

ドーム — Dōmu

The temple carries two domes. The roof of the main hall is a beautiful half-sphere modelled on the oldest form of Buddhist stupa — the symbol of the temple as a Buddhist sanctuary.

On the roof of the five-storey wing stands a second dome — a silver skeletal frame modelled on the Hiroshima Atomic Bomb Dome.

Dove Relief

ハトのレリーフ — Hato no Relīfu

A sculpted relief runs across two exterior walls between the fourth and fifth floors: six panels showing a missile (war, armament) gradually transforming into a dove (peace). With the skeletal dome above, this is the temple's visible vow for peace.

Peace Declaration Stone

平和宣言碑 — Heiwa Sengen-hi

On 13 April 1986, at the general assembly held during the founding anniversary, the congregation adopted the Myohokkein Anti-Nuclear Peace Declaration. Four years later, in December 1990, a stone carved with the full text was installed in the planting bed beside the main gate.

Koi Garden

鯉庭 — Koi-niwa

A small pond set within the inner courtyard, with carp circling among the stones — a place to pause and let the morning pass slowly before climbing on.

Founder's Hall

祖師堂 — Soshi-dō

The final stop on the walk — a quiet hall honouring the founder, Nichiren Shōnin, and the generations of priests who have tended this temple.
